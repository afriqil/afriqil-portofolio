What I Did

Large-scale SQL analytics workflow.

I queried the NYC Yellow Taxi public dataset directly in Google BigQuery and audited 30.9 million raw trip records. Before analysis, I inspected the schema, compared available annual tables, verified monthly coverage, and selected 2021 because it provided a complete January-to-December period.

I built a reusable cleaned analytical view that removed invalid timestamps, nonpositive distance and monetary values, and operationally implausible outliers. The final view retained 30,222,103 trips, or 97.79% of the raw records, while preserving zero and null passenger counts because passenger volume was not required for the main analysis.

Using CTEs, conditional aggregation, date functions, SAFE_DIVIDE, APPROX_QUANTILES, joins, LAG, and ROW_NUMBER, I analyzed monthly and hourly demand, weekday-versus-weekend behavior, pickup zones, directional routes, payment methods, and credit-card tipping. I exported only aggregated results to CSV, created four supporting Excel visuals, and documented cleaning rules, data definitions, and interpretation limitations.

Key Insights

What the SQL analysis revealed.

November recorded the highest demand

November reached 3.41 million trips, averaged 113,724 trips per day, and generated approximately USD 70.66 million in recorded transaction value.

Weekday and weekend peaks differed

Weekday demand peaked at 18:00 with 6,328 average trips per day for that hour, while weekend demand peaked earlier at 15:00 with 5,071.

Trip volume and transaction value told different stories

Upper East Side South had the highest pickup volume at 1.54 million trips, while JFK Airport produced the highest total recorded amount at USD 58.20 million.

Short Manhattan routes dominated route activity

Upper East Side South to Upper East Side North recorded 224,721 trips and the highest route-level recorded amount at approximately USD 2.66 million.

Card tips increased in dollars but declined as a fare ratio

Credit cards represented 73.42% of cleaned trips. Across card trips, average tip amount increased with distance, while the aggregate tip-to-fare ratio declined from 32.17% to 17.24%.

Business Recommendations

Operational implications from the analysis.

Match fleet positioning to day-type demand

Use separate weekday and weekend schedules because the strongest pickup periods occur at different hours, with weekday demand peaking later.

Separate volume and transaction-value strategies

Treat high-frequency Manhattan zones and high-value airport zones as different operating segments rather than ranking locations with one metric.

Use volume thresholds with efficiency metrics

Evaluate amount-per-minute or amount-per-mile only alongside trip volume, distance, and duration so short trips with small denominators do not dominate decisions.

Interpret tipping results as card-only behavior

Use the tipping findings for recorded credit-card transactions and avoid comparing them directly with cash tips, which may not be fully captured.

Add external operational data before deployment

Combine the trip data with taxi supply, traffic, weather, event, airport-queue, and empty-mile information before making driver-profit or fleet-allocation claims.

Technical Highlights

Technical work behind the SQL case study.

Large-Scale BigQuery SQL

Queried and analyzed more than 30 million public trip records directly in BigQuery without downloading the raw dataset.

Data Quality and Outlier Auditing

Used COUNTIF, APPROX_QUANTILES, timestamp calculations, and candidate thresholds to quantify and document invalid and extreme records.

Window Functions and Trend Analysis

Applied LAG and ROW_NUMBER to calculate month-over-month growth and identify leading months, hours, zones, and routes.

Dimension Join Validation

Detected duplicate taxi-zone IDs, deduplicated the reference table, measured a 99.08% pickup-zone match rate, and prevented one-to-many row inflation.

Reproducible SQL Documentation

Organized ten SQL scripts, seven result exports, cleaning rules, a data dictionary, limitations, and a repository-level analytical README.
